There is a real first-mover advantage for companies that can leverage current generation LLM tools. There is also a real training and implementation challenge: the highest-value capabilities are still at the bleeding edge. Practices are unstandardized, protocols change constantly, and any integration-dependent system can be made obsolete overnight.

My approach centers on a two-tiered curriculum model. The first is a hierarchy of use cases: simple prompting and research; language drafting and content generation; workflow improvements using connectors; out-of-the-box agentic LLM tools; built-in integrations and automation; and customized LLM-orchestrated architectures. These categories will likely persist. Tiers one through three are fairly stable, but tiers four through six are moving fast enough that any specific how-to curriculum at those levels will probably be obsolete within six weeks. For instance, Claude's desktop application has been updated over six times in the past week. Any one of those updates could deprecate a workflow someone paid thousands of dollars to build.

The second idea addresses that directly: rather than teaching staff what to do at each level, teach them how to use LLMs to figure out what to do next as the technology changes. That means helping them think more like no-code programmers, and helping them understand that LLMs make that accessible to anyone.

Structurally, this is two to six progressive modules covering LLM basics for day-to-day productivity, with case studies drawn from real use cases at your company. A pivotal module on prompt design principles, drawing from a prompt engineering reference I've developed and included as a portfolio item for you to review, marks the transition from basic to advanced training.

Learning prompt design principles requires a real cognitive investment, but it yields outsized value. Not all of your staff will need or want to go that deep, and I'd recommend against pressuring them to. Some people have genuine personal reservations about AI, and a tiered opt-in model respects that. The practical upside is that a few enthusiastic, advanced LLM users can unlock a lot of value for the broader team if you build that into your implementation plan.

The later modules cover how connectors and integrations work in more detail, with structured projects that are LLM-guided rather than step-by-step instructional. Instead of "do this, then that," participants are instructed to design a prompt that guides them through researching, designing, and implementing a workflow to automate a real use case in their own job. That's what makes the training durable.

Most of my training and facilitation experience has been in adjacent areas: team-building, group communication skills, clinical case presentations, and ongoing project coordination. I use a Tell-Show-Do model: explain, demonstrate, then support participants as they practice on their own. I keep everything grounded in realistic scenarios.

For 15-20 people, I'd run a short pre-survey to assess skill levels, run early modules in separate cohorts, and reconvene once there's a shared foundation. Separating early keeps both ends engaged without slowing the group down.

My hourly rate is $65. One question before I sketch out a more detailed structure: what format are you envisioning for the deliverables — live sessions, async modules, a facilitator guide, or some combination?
